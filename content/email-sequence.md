## EMAIL 4 — Day 5
**Subject:** Why fermented food works better than probiotics (most of the time)
**Preview:** It's not the bacteria. It's what the bacteria produce.

---

A $60 probiotic capsule contains roughly 30–50 billion colony-forming units.

Your gut contains roughly 38 trillion microorganisms.

The math problem should be obvious.

What probiotics do — when they work — is introduce specific strains that compete with harmful bacteria and, in some cases, produce beneficial metabolites while they're passing through. Useful. But limited.

What fermentation does is different. The fermentation process itself changes the chemistry of the food. Lectins are partially degraded. Phytates are reduced. Short-chain fatty acids — particularly butyrate — are produced. Butyrate is the preferred fuel of colonocytes, the cells that line your colon. It directly supports tight junction integrity.

You're not introducing foreign bacteria into your gut. You're feeding the cells that build the wall.

Sauerkraut made with a three-day ferment does not have the same chemistry as sauerkraut that sat in a jar for four hours. The timing matters. The salt concentration matters. The temperature matters. Traditional fermentation understood this intuitively. Modern food production largely abandoned it in favor of shelf stability.

The protocol includes a fermentation primer. Not a recipe collection. The principles behind why it works — so you can apply them to anything.
